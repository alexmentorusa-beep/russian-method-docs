Distinct Cortical Areas Associated with Native and Second Languages

Karl H. S. Kim  ·  Norman R. Relkin  ·  Kyoung-Min Lee  ·  Joy Hirsch
Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center & Cornell University Medical College, New York, USA
Published in Nature fMRI Study 12 Bilingual Subjects 10 Languages Received: 20 Feb 1997 · Accepted: 30 Apr 1997
Abstract

The ability to acquire and use several languages selectively is a unique and essential human capacity. Using functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), the authors investigated how multiple languages are represented in the human cortex — and discovered that second languages acquired in adulthood are spatially separated from native languages in Broca's area, while languages learned simultaneously in early childhood share common frontal cortical areas.

Background

The Question of Multiple Languages in One Brain

Indirect evidence for topographic specialization within the language-dominant hemisphere of multilingual subjects had long existed: bilingual epilepsy patients occasionally lose the ability to speak one language while retaining another during seizures; stroke victims can permanently lose one language while preserving a second; and electrical stimulation of discrete neocortical regions can selectively disrupt individual languages in polyglots.

Yet the precise spatial relationship between different languages in the living brain had never been directly localized — until this study.

Such functions have not been localized — until now.

— Kim, Relkin, Lee & Hirsch, Nature (1997)
Anatomy

The Two Language Regions Under Study

Broca's Area

Located in the inferior frontal gyrus (Brodmann's areas 44 & 46). Responsible for the execution of speech and deep grammatical processing. This is the anterior language region — and the site of the study's most dramatic finding.

Wernicke's Area

Located in the posterior superior temporal gyrus (Brodmann's area 22). Responsible for understanding the meaning of words and the semantics of language. This posterior region showed little separation between languages regardless of acquisition age.

Imaging & Task Protocol

A 1.5-tesla MRI scanner with echo-planar imaging was used to acquire T2*-weighted images sensitive to changes in deoxyhaemoglobin levels accompanying neuronal activation. Sixteen contiguous brain slices covered both the inferior frontal gyrus (Broca's area) and the posterior superior temporal gyrus (Wernicke's area).

Subjects performed a silent sentence-generation task: they were asked to mentally "describe" events from the previous day (morning, afternoon, night) using complex sentences — first in one language, then the other. Graphical cues were displayed in unpredictable order to prevent mental rehearsal. Two identical runs were performed per language, and languages were alternated to prevent habituation.

Statistical analysis used a multistage coincidence method requiring significant signal changes across two independent runs, with an empirically determined false-positive rate of p ≤ 0.0005. The centroid of each language's activation cluster was calculated as its two-dimensional centre of mass.

Results

Key Findings

🧠
Late Bilinguals
Second language learned in adulthood
  • Two distinct Broca's areas — one per language
  • Centre-to-centre separation: 4.5–11.2 mm
  • Mean separation in Broca's area: 6.43 ± 1.83 mm
  • Zero shared voxels between the two language zones
  • Wernicke's area: centroids only 1.1–3.3 mm apart
🧠
Early Bilinguals
Two languages acquired in infancy
  • Languages intermingled in a single Broca's region
  • Mean separation in Broca's area: only 1.53 ± 0.78 mm
  • 20–62% of voxels common to both languages
  • Wernicke's area: mean separation 1.58 ± 0.79 mm
  • Both areas show effectively no language segregation
Statistical Confirmation (ANOVA)

Significant main effects were found for language area (p ≤ 0.000059) and bilingual type (p ≤ 0.000084), with a significant interaction (p ≤ 0.000067). This confirms that the separation in Broca's area is specific to late acquisition, while Wernicke's area shows minimal separation regardless of acquisition age.

Critical Asymmetry

The mean centroid distance in the anterior (Broca's) area for late bilinguals (6.43 mm) significantly exceeded the posterior (Wernicke's) distance (1.88 mm), with t = 5.43, d.f. = 5, p ≤ 0.004 — confirming that the effect is specific to the frontal language region, not a general brain-wide phenomenon.

Interpretation

What Does This Mean?

The observation that languages in Broca's area are spatially separated only when the second language was learned late in life suggests that the age of acquisition is a critical factor in the functional organization of this region. Human infants are initially capable of discriminating all phonetically relevant differences across languages — but gradually narrow their perceptual acoustic space through repeated early exposure.

It is possible that representations of languages in Broca's area, once established through early-life exposure, are not subsequently modified. This would force the brain to recruit adjacent cortical tissue for a second language learned as an adult — explaining both the spatial separation seen in late bilinguals and the well-documented difficulty of adult language acquisition.

Wernicke's area — responsible for semantic meaning — appears to remain flexible throughout life, allowing later-learned languages to share the same semantic processing substrate. The critical divide, therefore, is in phonetic and grammatical execution, not in conceptual meaning.

Representations of languages in Broca's area that are developed by exposure early in life are not subsequently modified — necessitating adjacent cortical areas for a second language learned as an adult.

— Authors' Interpretation
